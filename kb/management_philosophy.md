# Management Philosophy

## How I build teams

I really like analysts who are curious. I don't care about their skill set coming in — technical tools I can teach. I can't teach curiosity: curiosity of data, curiosity of process, curiosity of anything — of how to improve and get better, of asking why a process exists. A specific hire I took a flyer on was an analyst who was smart and highly recommended internally, but his current job had nothing to do with what he'd be doing on my team. What stood out was the stuff he was doing on his current team — trying to make it a little bit better, a little bit more efficient, always improving a process. On the flip side, one thing I got wrong was keeping too long a leash on a contingent contractor because her responsibilities were limited — multiple mistakes and missed deliveries could have been avoided if I'd addressed the pattern and let her go earlier than I did. One thing I'll always push for as a boss: making sure my analysts are compensated appropriately. At Under Armour, I had an analyst who was severely underpaid because he'd been there so long — other analysts, hired after him and doing less work, were paid more. I thought it was unfair. I fought for him to be the highest-paid employee on my team, and got it done.

## How I coach and give feedback

I think giving feedback is a skill, and it's not one that comes innate to me. Early on, my boss passed down skip-level feedback that I was too direct — that I needed to land bad news softer with cross-functional partners. I've worked on it, but the directness itself is fair. It has nothing to do with disrespect; it has everything to do with how my brain is wired and how things piece together to make sense to me. If I don't understand something, I get curious and less defensive. If someone's encroaching on an area I know specifics about, I get direct.

I do biweekly 1:1s with analysts — they set the agenda, I ask for the last five minutes to cover staff items. I only send written feedback via email in two cases: when it's really, really negative and you've missed my social cues via verbal feedback, or when it's really, really positive — usually when the team has just shipped something and I want to give public props.

## How I handle disagreement

The lesson on internal pushback as a PM: I need to prioritize when it makes sense to escalate to leadership versus taking the path of least resistance. That lesson cost some tech debt recently — and next time, what's it going to cost us? Ultimately, I need to be more assertive and know when escalation is the right call. The other thing I've really worked on in disagreements is pulling individuals offline — out of large conference calls, into direct conversation over coffee or a quick one-on-one at each other's desks. Taking a disagreement offline prevents you from sounding like a jerk publicly, and it can unlock knowledge you didn't have before. People are also less likely to sound overly critical over email than in a meeting — and in-meeting critique is almost always a distraction from the meeting goal. Offline usually resolves the issue without dragging people who don't have the context into it.

## How I run requirements intake

The reason I run intake this way: it provides accountability on both sides. I'm held accountable to what was explained and reviewed with me, and the business is accountable for what gets worked on.

The artifact is complete when the definition of done is satisfied — that could be as simple as a button on an interface, or as involved as building out the workflow for a NAV package deliverable. Our initial intake form captures expectations for the new feature and the non-functional requirements that go with it. I review the results and create the Jira artifacts from there.

All requirements get reviewed with my team first, then confirmed in a refinement session. Once we're in agreement, I pass the acceptance criteria to the requester over email and ask them to confirm. Once that's received, we add it to the next appropriate sprint that has time available. The sign-off is so that when something goes wrong or someone complains to leadership, we have documented proof of what was asked for.

## How I read product success signals

The best signal is silence. My favorite example was my second Black Friday while working in retail. I had helped support reporting the year before, but this was the first year I owned the intake on the reporting package. I changed the frequency and the source testing to resolve previous year's cataloged issues — and through that, I cut support tickets for flash-report issues by 75%.

On executive-level dashboards I've delivered, I always built in an audit component to answer adoption-metric questions when they came up. For major releases, I ran a release-notes meeting with the leaders whose teams the product affected — a show-and-tell to get them excited.
